## Can You Use an AirTag as a Pet Tracker?

Let's be direct: the Apple AirTag is not a pet tracker. Apple designed it to find your keys, wallet, or luggage. But at $29 with no subscription, pet owners everywhere have been strapping them to collars and hoping for the best. So we did the same — here's what actually happens when you use an AirTag to track a dog.

## How AirTags Work (And Why It Matters for Pets)

AirTags don't have GPS in the traditional sense. They use Bluetooth to communicate with nearby Apple devices (iPhones, iPads, Macs). When any Apple device passes near your AirTag, it anonymously relays the location to your Find My app. The more Apple devices nearby, the more frequent your updates.

This is fundamentally different from a dedicated pet GPS tracker that uses cellular networks to report its location every few seconds. With an AirTag, you only get a location update when someone with an iPhone walks or drives within Bluetooth range of your pet.

## Real-World Test Results

**In a suburban neighborhood:** Updates came every 5-20 minutes. Enough iPhones are walking around, driving by, and sitting in homes that the AirTag's position refreshed reasonably often. When our dog escaped into the neighborhood, we got a location pin within about 8 minutes.

**In a rural area:** Practically useless. We tested during a hike in a state park and got zero updates for over two hours. No nearby iPhones means no location reports. If your dog runs into woods or farmland, the AirTag won't help.

**At a dog park:** Updates were frequent (every 2-3 minutes) because dozens of iPhones were present. But you could also just... look at your dog.

## What You Don't Get

No real-time tracking. No escape alerts. No virtual fences. No activity monitoring. No health data. You can't watch your dog move on a map in real time. You get a static pin that says "last seen here at this time" — and that's it.

The AirTag also has no way to notify you proactively if your pet leaves an area. You have to open the app and check. By the time you realize your dog is gone and check Find My, they could be anywhere.

## The Precision Finding Feature

If you're within Bluetooth range (about 30 feet), the UWB chip in newer iPhones gives you directional arrows and distance to the AirTag. This is genuinely useful for the final stage of finding a lost pet — once you're in the right area, it guides you right to them. But you need to get close first.

## When an AirTag Is Enough

An AirTag works as a pet "tracker" if: your pet only goes missing in dense suburban or urban areas, you just want a last-resort backup alongside a secure collar, or your cat occasionally escapes but stays in the neighborhood. Think of it as a better-than-nothing safety net, not a tracking system.

## When You Need a Real Tracker

If your dog is an escape artist, you hike in remote areas, you need instant alerts when they leave the yard, or you have an outdoor cat in a less populated area — spend the extra money on a Tractive or Whistle. Real-time cellular tracking versus crowd-sourced Bluetooth is a night-and-day difference for actually finding a lost pet.

## The Verdict

The AirTag is a $29 gamble. In the right environment (dense suburban areas with lots of iPhones), it provides a useful backup layer for finding a lost pet. In the wrong environment, it's a decorative collar charm. It's not a replacement for dedicated GPS trackers — but for indoor cats who occasionally dart out the door, or as a supplement to other safety measures, the price is right.
